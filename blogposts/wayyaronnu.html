A Curious Word in Leviticus 9

April 12, 2026

In last week's Torah portion, we read the following verse:

ותֵּ֤צֵא אֵשׁ֙ מִלִּפְנֵ֣י יְקוָ֔ק וַתֹּ֙אכַל֙ עַל־הַמִּזְבֵּ֔חַ אֶת־הָעֹלָ֖ה וְאֶת־הַחֲלָבִ֑ים וַיַּ֤רְא כׇּל־הָעָם֙ וַיָּרֹ֔נּוּ וַֽיִּפְּל֖וּ עַל־פְּנֵיהֶֽם׃
Leviticus 9:24

The Hebrew word וַיָּרֹ֔נּוּ (vayaronnu) stood out to me when I read through the portion. God sends out a flame to consume the material on the altar, and the nation is רנן and falls upon their faces. I usually associate this word with shouts of joy or music, as it appears often in Psalms. For instance

נְהָרוֹת יִמְחֲאוּ כָף יַחַד הָרִים יְרַנֵּנוּ
Leviticus 9:24

Onkelos handles this difficulty by translating the verb as "praise:"

וּנְפָקַת אֶשָּׁתָא מִן קֳדָם יְיָ וַאֲכָלַת עַל מַדְבְּחָא יָת עֲלָתָא וְיָת תַּרְבַּיָּא וַחֲזָא כָּל עַמָּא וְשַׁבָּחוּ וּנְפָלוּ עַל אַפֵּיהוֹן:
Targum Onkelos on Leviticus 9:24

Rashi assents to Onkelos' interpretation, commenting כתרגומו. Moreover, if we look at how he takes the prior verse,

וַיָּבֹ֨א מֹשֶׁ֤ה וְאַהֲרֹן֙ אֶל־אֹ֣הֶל מוֹעֵ֔ד וַיֵּ֣צְא֔וּ וַֽיְבָרְכ֖וּ אֶת־הָעָ֑ם וַיֵּרָ֥א כְבוֹד־יקוק אֶל־כׇּל־הָעָֽם׃
Leviticus 9:23

he writes

אָמְרוּ וִיהִי נֹעַם ה' אלקינו עָלֵינוּ, יְהִי רָצוֹן שֶׁתִּשְׁרֶה שְׁכִינָה בְמַעֲשֵׂה יְדֵיכֶם; לְפִי שֶׁכָּל שִׁבְעַת יְמֵי הַמִּלּוּאִים שֶׁהֶעֱמִידוֹ מֹשֶׁה לַמִּשְׁכָּן וְשִׁמֵּשׁ בּוֹ וּפֵרְקוֹ בְכָל יוֹם לֹא שָׁרְתָה בוֹ שְׁכִינָה, וְהָיוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל נִכְלָמִים וְאוֹמְרִים לְמֹשֶׁה, מֹשֶׁה רַבֵּנוּ, כָּל הַטֹּרַח שֶׁטָּרַחְנוּ שֶׁתִּשְׁרֶה שְׁכִינָה בֵינֵינוּ וְנֵדַע שֶׁנִּתְכַּפֵּר לָנוּ עֲוֹן הָעֵגֶל , לְכָךְ אָמַר לָהֶם זֶה הַדָּבָר אֲשֶׁר צִוָּה ה' תַּעֲשׂוּ וְיֵרָא אֲלֵיכֶם כְּבוֹד ה' — אַהֲרֹן אָחִי כְּדַאי וְחָשׁוּב מִמֶּנִּי, שֶׁעַל יְדֵי קָרְבְּנוֹתָיו וַעֲבוֹדָתוֹ תִשְׁרֶה שְׁכִינָה בָכֶם וְתֵדְעוּ שֶׁהַמָּקוֹם בָּחַר בּוֹ:
Rashi on Leviticus 9:23

One could surmise that Rashi is reading in a sense of almost relief on the part of the Israelites, as they are finally witnessing a mechanism by which they can repent of their sin of the golden calf.

Robert Alter roughly agrees with Rashi and Onkelos' sense, translating

And a fire came out from before the LORD and consumed on the altar the burnt offering and the fat, and all the people saw and shouted with joy and fell on their faces.
Alter on Leviticus 9:23

However, the usage of the verb here may be more nuanced and polysemous than it first seems. The BDB entry for the root r-n-n claim the root is onomatopoeic in origin and cognate to an Arabic word رن (ranna) that has to do with a cry aloud, or the twanging sound of a bowstring. It then refers the reader to an illuminating verse from Lamentations:

ק֣וּמִי ׀ רֹ֣נִּי (בליל) [בַלַּ֗יְלָה] לְרֹאשׁ֙ אַשְׁמֻר֔וֹת שִׁפְכִ֤י כַמַּ֙יִם֙ לִבֵּ֔ךְ נֹ֖כַח פְּנֵ֣י אֲדֹנָ֑י שְׂאִ֧י אֵלָ֣יו כַּפַּ֗יִךְ עַל־נֶ֙פֶשׁ֙ עֽוֹלָלַ֔יִךְ הָעֲטוּפִ֥ים בְּרָעָ֖ב בְּרֹ֥אשׁ כׇּל־חוּצֽוֹת׃
Lamentations 2:19

The daughter of Zion is being urged to רנן. However, the surrounding context, and indeed the book of Lamentations in its entirety, she is being urged to cry and lament. The verse is imbued with distress and other negative sentiments, not positive ones like shouts of joy.

Another example of this secondary meaning is found in 1 Kings. A large r-n-n passes through the Israelite encampment at the announcement of the death of their king Ahab in battle:

וַיַּעֲבֹ֤ר הָֽרִנָּה֙ בַּֽמַּחֲנֶ֔ה כְּבֹ֥א הַשֶּׁ֖מֶשׁ לֵאמֹ֑ר אִ֥ישׁ אֶל־עִיר֖וֹ וְאִ֥ישׁ אֶל־אַרְצֽוֹ׃
1 Kings 22:36

This is a strong proof that the root retains a secondary meaning other than a shout of joy in Biblical Hebrew, a meaning having to do not necessarily with mourning, but surprise, distress, or even a sort of fear.

The Septuagint translators agreed with this sense. They write

καὶ ἐξῆλθε πῦρ παρὰ Κυρίου καὶ κατέφαγε τὰ ἐπὶ τοῦ θυσιαστηρίου, τά τε ὁλοκαυτώματα καὶ τὰ στέατα, καὶ εἶδε πᾶς ὁ λαὸς καὶ ἐξέστη καὶ ἔπεσαν ἐπὶ πρόσωπον.
Septuagint Leviticus 9:23

The verb under focus is ἐξιστημι. It is compound and built out of a preposition ἐκ ("out") and root verb ἱστημι ("set, place"). The LSJ gives the semantic development of this root as meaning to drive out of one's mind; i.e. to be distressed, and that is likely the intended meaning here.

All in all, the dual meaning of this verb is possibly intentional. It encapsulates both the joy the Israelites experienced at the sight, as well as the unnerving awe that overcame them, causing them to subsequently fall on their faces in respect—one does not do such a thing if they are rejoicing. That duality is central to the Jewish tradition, and is found in many places through the pentateuch including in Deuteronomy:

וְעַתָּה֙ יִשְׂרָאֵ֔ל מָ֚ה יקוק אלקיך שֹׁאֵ֖ל מֵעִמָּ֑ךְ כִּ֣י אִם־לְ֠יִרְאָ֠ה אֶת־יקוק אלקיך לָלֶ֤כֶת בְּכׇל־דְּרָכָיו֙ וּלְאַהֲבָ֣ה אֹת֔וֹ וְלַֽעֲבֹד֙ אֶת־יקוק אלקיך בְּכׇל־לְבָבְךָ֖ וּבְכׇל־נַפְשֶֽׁךָ׃
Deuteronomy 10:12
